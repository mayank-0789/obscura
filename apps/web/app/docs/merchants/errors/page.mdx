# Error handling

The middleware handles x402 errors itself — a malformed payment becomes
a 402 with an explanation body, a failed settlement becomes a 402 with
`settle_failed`. Your handler never runs on a broken payment.

Exceptions bubble into Express's `next(err)` so you can catch them in a
global error handler:

<CodeBlock lang="ts" filename="server.ts">
{`app.use((err: unknown, _req, res, _next) => {
  console.error("[paid-route]", err);
  res.status(500).json({ error: "internal" });
});`}
</CodeBlock>

## Why 402 instead of 500 for settle failures

The client is better equipped to handle payment failures than your
server is. A 402 with `settle_failed` is an actionable response: the
client knows the signature was valid but settlement didn't land, and
can choose to retry with a fresh signature. A 500 would hide that
detail and trigger the wrong recovery path.

## Logging

Settlement failures + malformed payments are logged to `stderr` by
PayAI's underlying x402-solana library. For production you'll want to:

1. Forward these to your log aggregator (Datadog, Axiom, Sentry).
2. Alert on sustained `settle_failed` rates — indicates facilitator or
   RPC problems.
3. Ignore `invalid_payment` noise — clients trying random payloads is
   normal.

## What never reaches your handler

Your handler only runs after a confirmed, on-chain settlement. So you
never need to handle:

- Unsigned requests (→ 402 with quote).
- Invalid signatures (→ 402 with `invalid_payment`).
- Expired nonces or blockhashes (→ 402 with `invalid_payment`).
- Settlement failures (→ 402 with `settle_failed`).
- Wrong network / mint mismatches (→ 402 with `invalid_payment`).

If your handler runs, the payment is in your wallet.
